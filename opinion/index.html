Standing still is a choice.
And it is costing Scugog.

Scugog does not have to become another city. But refusing to plan responsibly for growth could make life more expensive for the people already living here.

The Scugog Scoop Editorial • July 15, 2026 • 5 minute read

For years, one phrase has surfaced whenever major housing or development proposals are discussed in Scugog:

“We do not want to become another Oshawa.”

That concern comes from a genuine place. People value Port Perry and the surrounding communities because they feel different. Scugog has a historic downtown, an active waterfront, productive farmland, rural landscapes and a strong sense of community.

Those qualities are worth protecting.

But protecting what makes Scugog special and refusing to grow are not the same thing.

Our community is already changing. Young families are struggling to find homes they can afford. Local businesses continue to search for employees. Roads, parks, recreation facilities and emergency services become more expensive to maintain every year.

The costs facing the municipality continue to increase whether the population grows or not.

The cost of doing nothing

When municipal expenses rise, there are only so many ways to pay for them.

Property taxes can increase. Services can be reduced. Projects can be delayed. Or the tax base can grow so that more households and businesses share the cost of maintaining the community.

Growth is not free. New homes require infrastructure. Roads, intersections, parks, trails, water systems, recreation facilities and emergency services all require careful planning and investment.

But new development can also bring new taxpayers, new customers for local businesses, new workers and new economic activity.

That additional assessment can help spread municipal costs across a broader tax base instead of placing an ever-growing burden on the residents who already live here.

Growth should not happen at any cost. Every proposal must still be judged on infrastructure, traffic, environmental protection, community design and long-term value.

Responsible growth requires scrutiny

Supporting responsible growth does not mean supporting every proposal exactly as it is presented.

Every major development deserves careful examination.

Traffic matters. Wetlands and natural heritage matter. School capacity matters. Servicing capacity matters. Housing type, density, public space and neighbourhood design all matter.

Residents should expect developers to answer difficult questions. They should also expect the municipality to clearly explain the costs, benefits and long-term consequences of its decisions.

But rejecting development simply because it is development is not a complete planning strategy.

It is avoidance.

We need better questions

Too often, conversations about growth begin and end with whether a project should be stopped.

Scugog should be asking more useful questions.

  • Can new neighbourhoods be walkable?
  • Can they include parks, trails and gathering spaces?
  • Can infrastructure be built before major problems appear?
  • Can new development support local shops and strengthen the downtown?
  • Can Scugog protect farmland and natural areas while still creating places for the next generation to live?
  • Can a broader tax base help maintain services without placing the entire burden on existing homeowners?

These are planning questions.

These are conversations worth having.

Scugog should shape growth, not fear it

Scugog does not need to abandon its identity in order to welcome new residents.

Growth can be designed around the character of the community. Buildings can be appropriately scaled. Natural features can be protected. Streets can be safer. Housing can be more diverse. Public spaces can be improved.

None of that happens automatically.

It requires strong municipal planning, meaningful public consultation and a willingness to negotiate better outcomes.

The choice facing Scugog is not simply between growth and no growth.

The real choice is between growth that is carefully shaped and change that happens without a clear long-term vision.

The easiest answer is not always the best one

Saying no can feel like the safest choice. It avoids immediate disruption and preserves the appearance that nothing has changed.

But standing still has consequences.

It can mean higher taxes for existing residents. It can mean fewer housing options for young people. It can mean a smaller customer base for local businesses. It can mean delaying infrastructure improvements until they become more expensive and more urgent.

Scugog should not approve growth without conditions, scrutiny or public input.

It should, however, recognize that refusing to plan for the future is itself a decision.

And it may ultimately be the most expensive decision the community can make.
